# Colorado Totes — Sales Call Script

> **Prepared for:** Rob Warren
> **Voice profile:** Applied (WritersRoom.ai VPD — 2026-03-30)
> **Date:** 2026-04-13
> **Target buyer:** Community Manager / Property Manager at 200+ unit Denver metro apartment complexes
> **Call objective:** Gauge interest → book a 15-minute in-person walk-through or pilot conversation

---

## Pre-Call Checklist

- [ ] Know the complex name, unit count, and management company before dialing
- [ ] Check the complex's current concession offers (Apartments.com, their website) — note the dollar amount if visible
- [ ] Know whether you're calling the on-site Community Manager or a Regional PM
- [ ] Have the one-pager ready to email immediately if they ask (or if you get "send me something")
- [ ] Write down the person's name and title — use it twice in the first 30 seconds

---

## MODULE A: COLD OUTBOUND

### A1. Opening (first 15 seconds)

**Variant 1 — Direct and honest:**
"Hey [Name], this is Rob Warren. I'll be straight with you — you don't know me, and this is a cold call. I'm working on something specific to apartment communities in Denver and I think it's worth 60 seconds of your time. Fair?"

**Variant 2 — Research-led:**
"Hi [Name], Rob Warren. I was looking at [Complex Name] — saw you're running [concession / free month / move-in special]. I had an idea about that and wanted to run it by you. Got a minute?"

**Variant 3 — Peer-to-peer:**
"[Name], Rob Warren calling. I'm a Denver guy building a new service for apartment communities, and I specifically wanted to talk to you because [Complex Name] is exactly the kind of property this was designed for. Quick question before I take any more of your time?"

### A2. Permission Bridge (next 15 seconds)

**Variant 1:**
"Here's what I'm not going to do — I'm not going to pitch you for 10 minutes. Can I ask you one question about how you handle move-ins, and if it's not relevant, I'm off your phone in 30 seconds?"

**Variant 2:**
"I know your day is packed. Let me ask one thing — if it doesn't land, I'll let you go. Fair?"

**Variant 3:**
"I want to make sure this is even worth your time. Can I describe what we do in two sentences, and you tell me if it's interesting or not?"

### A3. Discovery Questions (SPIN sequence)

#### Situation Questions
*(Pick one — you should already know most of this from research)*

- "When a new tenant signs a lease at [Complex Name], what does the move-in experience look like from your side? Do you offer anything to help with the actual move?"
- "I'm curious — how are you differentiating [Complex Name] from the other buildings competing for the same renters right now?"

#### Problem Questions

- "What are you hearing from tenants about the move-in process? Any friction there?"
- "With vacancy where it is right now in Denver, how much pressure are you feeling on concessions? Are you finding that the free-month offers are actually moving the needle, or is everyone doing the same thing?"
- "When tenants are comparing you to the building down the street, what's the conversation you wish you were having with them?"

#### Implication Questions

- "If every complex on the block is offering a free month of rent, what does that do to your margins over 50, 100 move-ins a year?"
- "When a tenant has a rough move-in experience — cardboard everywhere, disorganized, stressful — does that affect how they talk about the building to friends? Does it show up in reviews?"
- "What happens to your renewal rate if the first impression of the building is chaotic?"

#### Need-Payoff Questions

- "If you could offer something that actually made the move-in feel premium — not just a discount, but an experience — would that change the conversation with prospective tenants?"
- "Would it help if there was a move-in amenity that cost a fraction of a free month's rent but made your building stand out in a way nobody else is doing?"
- "If tenants were literally carrying your building's brand through competing complexes on move-in day — would that be worth something to you?"

### A4. Value Bridge

**Variant 1 — After they've expressed concession fatigue:**
"That's exactly what I keep hearing. Everyone's throwing free rent at the problem, which costs you $1,700 a move and doesn't differentiate you at all. Here's what we do — Colorado Totes delivers branded plastic moving totes to your incoming tenants two weeks before move-in. The totes say 'I'm headed to [Complex Name]' on the side. Tenant uses them to move, drops them at a centralized spot in your building, we pick them up. Your cost is about $100 a move — roughly 6% of what a free month costs — and your brand is physically traveling through competing buildings on move-in day."

**Variant 2 — Shorter, after they've confirmed the pain:**
"So what we built is simple. We deliver branded plastic totes to your incoming tenants, they move with them, and they drop them off at a designated spot in your building. We handle everything. You get a premium move-in amenity at $100 a move instead of $1,700 — and your name is literally on totes moving through other buildings."

**Variant 3 — For the skeptical / time-pressed PM:**
"Look — $100 a move. We deliver totes branded with your complex name to incoming tenants. They move, drop them off in your building, we pick up. You spend 6 cents on the dollar compared to a free month of rent, and you get something nobody else in Denver is offering. That's the whole thing."

### A5. Social Proof Drop

**Pre-validation proof (honest — you're early):**
"I'll be straight — we're early. I'm specifically reaching out to a handful of the best-run communities in Denver to test this. I've already confirmed that nobody else is doing B2B tote rental in this market — the closest thing is a consumer service called Stack that charges tenants $130 out of pocket and doesn't touch the apartment channel. This is an open lane."

**Market proof:**
"Denver's at 7.6% vacancy — 16-year high. Complexes are spending $676 per unit per year in concessions. The math on this is hard to argue with — a fraction of that budget buys a move-in experience that actually differentiates."

### A6. Call to Action

**Variant 1 — In-person:**
"Here's what I'd like to do. I'm local — can I come by [Complex Name] for 15 minutes this week, show you a tote with your branding mocked up, and walk you through the logistics? If it doesn't make sense after that, no hard feelings."

**Variant 2 — Low-commitment:**
"Can I send you a one-page breakdown and then grab 15 minutes on the phone to walk through it? I can have it in your inbox in five minutes."

**Variant 3 — Regional play:**
"I know [Management Company] has [X] properties in Denver. If this works for [Complex Name], it could roll across the portfolio. Would it make sense to loop in your regional PM for a quick conversation?"

## MODULE C: OBJECTION BANK

### C1. Price / Budget

**Objection:** "We don't really have budget for another vendor service."

**Response 1 (Reframe):**
"Totally get it. Here's the math that might change how you think about it — you're already spending roughly $1,750 per move-in on concessions. This is $100. The question isn't whether you have budget for it — it's whether $100 buys you more differentiation than the $1,700 you're already spending. In practice, it does."

**Response 2 (Boomerang):**
"That's actually why this exists. Concession budgets are getting crushed right now. This gives you a premium amenity at a fraction of what free rent costs — and unlike free rent, it's visible. Your name is on totes going through other buildings."

**Response 3 (Pilot de-risk):**
"What if we started with a 10-move pilot? That's about $1,000 total — less than one month's rent concession on a single unit. Low risk way to see if tenants respond."

### C2. Timing / Not Now

**Objection:** "This isn't really a priority for us right now."

**Response 1 (Implication pivot):**
"I hear you — and I know your plate is full. Let me ask this though: when does the next lease-up season hit for you? Because the complexes that have this in place before peak move-in season are the ones who get the differentiation benefit. If we wait until summer, we're setting up during the rush instead of before it."

**Response 2 (Market urgency):**
"Fair. One thing worth considering — Denver vacancy is at a 16-year high right now. That's the window where this move-in amenity has the most leverage. When the market tightens back up, you won't need to differentiate as hard. Right now you do."

**Response 3 (Soft persistence):**
"No pressure at all. Can I check back in two weeks? And in the meantime I'll send you the one-pager so you have it when timing makes sense."

### C3. Need to Think About It / Talk to Team

**Objection:** "Let me think about it and get back to you."

**Response 1 (Isolate):**
"Absolutely — take your time. Can I ask: is there a specific concern you're weighing, or is it more about finding the right moment? If there's something I can answer now, I'd rather save you the mental real estate."

**Response 2 (Advance anyway):**
"Of course. While you're thinking it over, would it help if I mocked up what a branded tote would look like with [Complex Name] on it? Sometimes seeing it makes the conversation easier with your team."

**Response 3 (Name the next step):**
"Totally fair. Can we put 15 minutes on the calendar for [specific day] so I can answer whatever comes up? That way it doesn't fall off your radar and I'm not chasing you."

### C4. Already Have a Solution

**Objection:** "We already offer moving assistance / we work with a moving company."

**Response 1 (Differentiate):**
"That's great — means you already see the value of supporting the move-in experience. What we do is structurally different. This isn't a moving service — it's a branded amenity. The totes have your complex name on them. When a tenant moves from across town, everyone who sees those totes sees your brand. No moving company does that."

**Response 2 (Complement, don't replace):**
"This actually pairs with what you're doing — it doesn't replace it. The tote delivery happens two weeks before move-in. By the time the movers show up, the tenant already has their totes packed and ready. It makes the whole process smoother."

### C5. Send Me an Email

**Objection:** "Can you just send me an email with the details?"

**Response 1 (Earn one more question):**
"I will — happy to. Before I do, let me ask one thing so I can make it relevant to you: are you feeling more pressure on occupancy right now, or on concession costs? That'll help me tailor what I send."

**Response 2 (Agree + pin down follow-up):**
"Absolutely. I'll have it in your inbox in 10 minutes. Can I call you [specific day] to walk through any questions? I don't want it to just sit in a pile."

**Response 3 (Honest):**
"I can do that. I'll be straight though — emails about new vendor services tend to get buried. Can I get 90 more seconds right now? If it doesn't land, I won't follow up."

### C6. "Our Tenants Can Just Buy Boxes"

**Objection:** "Tenants can get boxes from Home Depot or U-Haul — why would we pay for this?"

**Response 1 (Reframe from tenant cost to PM value):**
"They absolutely can. But that's their problem, not yours. This isn't about saving tenants $30 on boxes — it's about your building offering something nobody else does. When a prospective tenant is comparing two buildings with the same price and the same free month, and one of them says 'we deliver moving totes branded with our name to your door two weeks before move-in' — that's a different conversation."

**Response 2 (Environmental angle):**
"Right — and those boxes get used once and go in the dumpster. Totes get reused hundreds of times. If your building has any sustainability messaging, this reinforces it. And it looks premium. Plastic totes stacked in a new apartment look different than crushed cardboard."

### C7. "How Do You Handle Tote Loss?"

**Objection:** "What if tenants don't return the totes?"

**Response:**
"Good question — it's the first thing everyone asks. Three layers. First, the totes are branded with your complex name, so they're not easy to repurpose. Second, we can include a credit card hold or small deposit at delivery. Third, pickup is centralized — tenants drop totes at a designated spot in your building, and your front desk or maintenance team can flag if someone hasn't returned them. In practice, loss rates on branded rental totes run 3-5% annually — and that's baked into our pricing already."

## MODULE D: QUICK REFERENCE

### Key Phrases Bank

1. "$100 a move vs. $1,750 for a free month — the math does the selling."
2. "Your brand, on totes, moving through competing buildings."
3. "This is a move-in amenity, not a moving service. Structurally different."
4. "We deliver, they move, they drop off, we pick up. You don't touch anything."
5. "6 cents on the dollar compared to free rent."
6. "Denver is at 7.6% vacancy — 16-year high. Every complex is competing for the same tenants."
7. "Nobody else in Denver is doing B2B tote rental. The lane is open."
8. "A 10-move pilot costs less than one month's free rent on a single unit."
9. "Centralized pickup — one stop per building, no matter how many tenants."
10. "The concession budget is already being spent. This just spends it smarter."
11. "When everyone's offering free rent, free rent stops being a differentiator."
12. "This works best for buildings that already run a tight operation."
13. "One regional yes could mean 20+ properties."
14. "The tote is the marketing. It moves through town with your name on it."

### Things to Never Say

1. **"We're disrupting the moving industry"** — you're not. You're a tote rental service. Keep it grounded.
2. **"Trust me, this works"** — nothing kills trust faster.
3. **"Our value proposition is..."** — Rob doesn't talk like this. Say what it does, plainly.
4. **"I'm sure you're aware that vacancy rates..."** — don't lecture PMs about their own market.
5. **"We're the first / only company doing this"** — say "nobody in Denver is doing this in the apartment channel." Specific, verifiable.
6. **"Does that make sense?"** — condescending. Replace with "What do you think?" or just pause.
7. **"I just wanted to check in"** — every follow-up needs new information or a specific question.

### Tonality Notes

**Opening 15 seconds:** Slightly slower than your natural pace. Confident, not rushed. You're a Denver entrepreneur calling about something specific — not a telemarketer burning through a list. Smile when you say their name. It sounds different and they can hear it.

**Discovery questions:** Genuinely curious tone. You're learning about their building, not setting traps. If they give you a surprising answer, react naturally — "Oh, interesting — tell me more about that."

**Value bridge:** Pick up the energy. This is where your conviction lands. You believe in this — let that come through. Short sentences. Direct. No hedging.

**Objection handling:** Stay level. Don't speed up or get defensive. Acknowledge first — always. Then respond with the same peer-to-peer energy you'd use explaining something to a friend.

**Close:** Casual confidence. You're proposing a next step, not begging for one. "Can I come by Thursday?" beats "Would there possibly be a time that might work for you this week?"
